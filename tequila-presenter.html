Additive-free means no caramel color, no oak extract, no glycerin, no sugar syrups. What you taste tonight is pure agave and oak—no shortcuts.

How to Taste

  • Nose gently: Hold the glass an inch away—tequila is higher proof than wine, so go easy
  • Open your mouth slightly while nosing—reduces alcohol burn, reveals aromas
  • Small sips: Let it coat your tongue. Notice sweetness, spice, warmth
  • Salt rim: Sip with salt first, then rotate the glass and try without—notice the difference
  • Water between pours to reset your palate

The Story of Tequila

The Blue Agave

Agave is not a cactus—it's a succulent related to lilies. Only Blue Weber agave (ah-GAH-vay) can be used for tequila. Each plant takes 6–8 years to mature, flowers once, then dies. The heart, called the piña (PEEN-yah), can weigh 80–200 lbs.

Highland agave (like Luna De Jalisco) produces sweeter, fruitier tequila. Valley agave (like Arette) produces earthier, more peppery tequila.

From Pulque to the World

~200 AD Aztecs ferment agave sap into pulque (POOL-keh)—a sacred ceremonial drink tied to the goddess Mayahuel, who had 400 rabbit children representing the infinite forms of drunkenness.
1500s Spanish conquistadors run out of brandy, apply distillation to agave. The town of Tequila, Jalisco (hah-LEES-ko) becomes the epicenter.
1758 The Cuervo family receives the first license to commercially produce tequila.
1974 Mexico establishes tequila's Denomination of Origin—only spirits from designated regions of Mexico using Blue Weber agave can legally be called tequila.
The 400 rabbits of Mayahuel: the Aztecs believed there were 400 kinds of drunk, each represented by a rabbit—from "happy rabbit" to "fighting rabbit." Ask the group which rabbit they plan to be tonight.

From Agave to Glass

How Tequila Is Made

  • Harvest: Expert jimadores (hee-mah-DOR-ays) cut the piña from the plant using a flat blade called a coa (KOH-ah)
  • Cook: Piñas are slow-cooked in brick ovens (hornos) or autoclaves to convert starch to sugar
  • Extract: Crushed to release the sweet juice—aguamiel (AH-gwah-myel) — "honey water"
  • Ferment: Yeast converts agave sugars into alcohol over 3–12 days
  • Distill: At least twice—copper pot stills add smoothness
  • Age: Blanco goes straight to bottle; reposado, añejo, and extra añejo rest in oak barrels

The Four Categories

Blanco / Silver

Unaged or up to 60 days • Pure agave expression • Crystal clear

Reposado (reh-poh-SAH-doh) — "Rested"

2–12 months in oak • Balanced agave + gentle vanilla • Pale gold

Añejo (ahn-YEH-ho) — "Aged"

1–3 years in oak • Rich caramel, spice, deep complexity • Amber

Extra Añejo

3+ years in oak • Whiskey-like depth, dark chocolate, dried fruit • Deep amber

The Specialty Salts

Each of the first three glasses has been lightly rimmed with a different salt. Salt suppresses bitterness, enhances sweetness, and opens up complex flavors in the spirit.

Lime Infused Salt → Luna De Jalisco Silver

The classic combination. Bright citrus salt amplifies the blanco's natural citrus and herbal character while tempering any peppery bite.

Maldon Sea Salt → Arette Reposado

Delicate, flaky pyramid crystals with a clean mineral finish. Maldon's subtlety won't overpower the reposado's gentle barrel notes—it enhances the agave's sweetness and lets the vanilla shine.

Smoked Chipotle Salt → Don Roberto Añejo

Bold meets bold. Smoky heat mirrors the deep barrel char and warming spice of a tequila aged two full years. Smoke, pepper, caramel, and oak weave together.

The Bad Stuff Extra Añejo gets no salt—after 3+ years in French oak, it earns the right to stand alone. Sipped neat.

Tell the group: "Sip where the salt rim is first, then rotate the glass and sip without. Notice how the flavor changes—salt literally rewires your taste buds to pick up things you'd otherwise miss."
Luna De Jalisco Silver / Blanco

Los Altos (Highland), Jalisco • 100% Single Estate Blue Agave • 40% ABV • Salt: Lime Infused

A blanco is the truest expression of agave—no barrel aging, just the plant and the distiller's craft. Luna De Jalisco uses open-air fermentation with native wild yeasts and is twice distilled in stainless steel pots with a copper coil. Starting here sets your baseline for everything that follows.

Appearance

Crystal clear with brilliant clarity

Nose

Sweet cooked agave, bright citrus, white pepper, green herbs, floral touch

Palate

Clean and crisp—sweet agave upfront, gentle peppery spice on the mid-palate, citrus and herbal notes. Light body, smooth texture.

Finish

Medium-length, lingering cooked agave sweetness, white pepper, clean floral fade

Pour first. Ask: "What's the first thing you notice on the nose?" Most will smell sweet agave and citrus. "This is what tequila actually smells like without any barrel influence. Memorize this—you'll taste it evolve with every pour after."
Arette Reposado Reposado

Town of Tequila (Valley), Jalisco • El Llano Distillery • Salt: Maldon Sea Salt

From the Orendain family—one of tequila's four founding families, making tequila since 1844. El Llano is the third-oldest distillery in the town of Tequila. Named after Arete, a legendary one-eyed show jumping horse that won Mexico its first Olympic gold in 1948. Agave slow-cooked in traditional brick ovens for 48 hours—no autoclaves, no shortcuts.

Appearance

Pale gold with a warm straw hue—the first visible evidence of barrel contact

Nose

Cooked agave, earthy minerality, light vanilla, caramel, dried herbs, peppery valley terroir

Palate

Balanced and smooth—agave still front and center but wrapped in gentle oak. Light vanilla, caramel sweetness, earthy undertones, cinnamon. Silky texture.

Finish

Medium-length, clean, sweet agave with a warm peppery fade. The oak whispers rather than shouts.

The horse story is memorable: "This tequila is named after a one-eyed horse that won Olympic gold." Also: "Remember the name Orendain—the next tequila, Don Roberto, comes from the same family. Two brands, same legendary bloodline."
Don Roberto Añejo Añejo

Jalisco • La Purísima Distillery • Aged 24 Months in White Oak • Salt: Smoked Chipotle

Founded by Don Roberto Orendain Jr. in 1995, building on a family tradition dating to 1924. Yes—the same Orendain family behind Arette. Different branch, different distillery, same legendary tequila bloodline. Produced with traditional stone and brick ovens, double distilled, then aged for a full 24 months in white oak barrels—right at the upper limit of the añejo category.

Appearance

Rich amber gold—dramatically deeper than the reposado. Thick, slow legs on the glass.

Nose

Roasted nuts, warm vanilla, caramel, oak, citrus peel, black pepper. Agave intertwined with deep barrel character.

Palate

Full-bodied and velvety. Rich caramel and vanilla, roasted agave, baking spices (cinnamon, nutmeg), toasted oak. Smooth, warming mouthfeel.

Finish

Long and warming with lingering oak, pepper, and a dry, bracing spice that invites the next sip

"You just tasted Arette from the Orendain family. Now Don Roberto—same family, different distillery. Like siblings raised in the same house who became completely different people." Ask: "Can you still taste the agave under all that oak? It's still there—the barrel builds around it, like a frame around a painting."
The Bad Stuff Extra Añejo

Los Altos, Jalisco • 3+ Years in French Oak • Double Distilled in Copper Pot Stills • Neat—No Salt

A three-generation family story. In the 1920s, Roberto Mares Sr. fled Mexico during the Cristero Revolution (kree-STEH-roh), arrived in the US during Prohibition, and sold handcrafted tequila to speakeasies in San Diego and Los Angeles. Today, Felipe Soto Mares—a certified Maestro Catador (Master Taster) and Maestro Tequilero (Master Distiller)—carries the family recipe. Aged a minimum of three years in French oak (tighter grain than American oak, releasing refined spice, nutmeg, and allspice notes more slowly).

Appearance

Deep amber to mahogany—the darkest pour of the night. Rich, viscous legs. Rivals aged whiskey in color.

Nose

Vanilla, butterscotch, caramelized agave, nutmeg, allspice, cinnamon, dried fruit, dark chocolate. New aromas emerge with each pass.

Palate

Rich and velvety with deep caramel, vanilla, baking spices, dark chocolate. French oak tannins are soft and integrated. Full body, silky, almost dessert-like.

Finish

Exceptionally long—warm waves of vanilla, spice, roasted agave, and oak that evolve for 30+ seconds

The showstopper. "The family literally fled a revolution, landed in America during Prohibition, and started selling tequila to speakeasies." After everyone sips: "That crystal-clear Luna De Jalisco and this deep amber pour started as the same thing: cooked agave juice. Time and oak did the rest."

Tequila Berry Smash

The Sweet & Tart Finale

To close out the evening, we're serving a Tequila Berry Smash—a vibrant cocktail that cleanses the palate after four straight pours.

Fresh muddled berries, lime juice, and a touch of sweetness—the berries provide sweet and tart fruit, the lime adds brightness, and the tequila ties it together with an agave backbone. Light, fun, and the perfect transition from a focused tasting to a celebration.
