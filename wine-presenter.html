Wine Tasting Tips

  • Hold by the Stem: Your hand warms the wine—keep the bowl cool
  • Swirl Gently: Opens up aromas by exposing wine to oxygen
  • Look First: Tilt glass against a white background to observe color
  • Cleanse Between Wines: Water and plain crackers reset your palate
  • Smell Before Sipping: 80% of what we "taste" is actually aroma
  • Small Sips: Let wine coat your tongue—front, sides, and back
  • Whites First: Always taste lightest to boldest

Wine: The Oldest Drink

Wine is among the oldest beverages ever created. Its story stretches back over 8,000 years—predating the wheel, writing, and even bread.

~6000 BC Earliest evidence of winemaking discovered in the Caucasus (modern Georgia). Clay vessels called qvevri (KWEV-ree) held fermented grape juice. Georgia still uses this method today—it's UNESCO-protected.
~3100 BC Ancient Egyptians cultivate vineyards along the Nile Delta. Wine becomes the drink of pharaohs—jars of wine were placed in tombs for the afterlife. King Tut was buried with 26 jars.
~800 BC Ancient Greeks spread viticulture across the Mediterranean. Dionysus (dy-oh-NY-sus) becomes the god of wine. The Greeks plant vines in southern Italy—calling it Oenotria (ee-NOH-tree-ah), "the land of wine."
~100 BC The Roman Empire industrializes wine. They plant vineyards across France, Spain, and Germany. Roman soldiers received wine rations daily—about a liter per person.
Italy was literally named "the land of wine" by the Greeks. Wine is in Italy's DNA—so it's fitting that two of tonight's wines come from there.

From Monks to the Modern World

500–1500 AD After Rome falls, Catholic monks preserve winemaking. Benedictine (ben-eh-DIK-tin) and Cistercian (sis-TER-shun) monasteries in Burgundy and Champagne perfect viticulture (VIT-ih-kul-chur) — grape growing, mapping the first terroir (tehr-WAHR)—the idea that soil, slope, and climate shape wine's character.
1600s–1700s Glass bottles and cork stoppers are invented, allowing wine to age for the first time. Dom Pérignon (dom pehr-een-YON) — Benedictine monk pioneers Champagne. Wine becomes a global trade commodity.
1860s The Great Wine Blight: the phylloxera (fil-LOX-er-ah) louse, accidentally brought from America, devastates nearly every vineyard in Europe—destroying 70% of France's vines. The solution? Grafting European vines onto resistant American rootstock. Almost every vine in the world today grows on American roots.
1976 The "Judgment of Paris": California wines beat France's best in a blind tasting, shocking the world and proving great wine can come from anywhere.
The phylloxera story is wild—America nearly destroyed all European wine, then saved it. And the Judgment of Paris is like a Cinderella story: nobody expected California to win, not even the Californians.

Old World vs. New World

Old World

Europe: France, Italy, Spain, Germany

  • Named by region (Chianti, Burgundy, Champagne)—not by grape
  • Emphasis on terroir—the land speaks through the wine
  • Tradition and strict regulations govern production
  • Tend toward subtlety, earthiness, and restraint

New World

Americas, Australia, NZ, South Africa

  • Named by grape variety (Pinot Noir, Chardonnay)
  • Emphasis on the winemaker's vision and innovation
  • Fewer regulations, more experimentation
  • Tend toward bolder fruit, riper flavors

Tonight's lineup bridges both worlds: Two Italian wines (Old World tradition), one New Zealand (New World innovation), and one Californian (New World terroir focus). You'll taste the difference in philosophy.

Ask the group: "When you pick up a bottle of Italian wine, you'll see a region name like 'Puglia' or 'Trentino.' A New Zealand bottle says 'Sauvignon Blanc' front and center. Same wine, different storytelling."

Why Is Red Wine Red?

The Science of Wine Color

The Secret Is in the Skin

Nearly all grape juice is clear or pale green—even from red grapes. If you squeeze a red grape, the juice inside is white.

Color comes from anthocyanins (an-tho-SY-uh-ninz)—pigment molecules in grape skins. The longer juice stays in contact with skins during fermentation (maceration (mass-er-AY-shun)), the deeper the color.

  • White wine: Juice pressed off skins immediately—no color
  • Rosé: Brief skin contact (hours to a day)—just a blush
  • Red wine: Days to weeks of skin contact—deep ruby to garnet

What Else the Skin Gives

Skin contact provides more than color:

  • Tannins: That dry, gripping sensation. Comes from skins, seeds, and stems. Gives red wine its structure and aging potential.
  • Body & texture: More skin contact = fuller, richer wine
  • Flavor compounds: Many complex flavors are extracted during maceration

Fun Fact: You can make white wine from red grapes! Champagne often includes Pinot Noir, pressed quickly to avoid color. It's called Blanc de Noirs (blahnk duh NWAHR)—"white from blacks."

Demo idea: if possible, show a red grape and squeeze it—the juice really is clear/white. It's a great visual. Also point to the Pinot Noir glass later and note how you can almost see through it (thin skins) vs. the Primitivo which is nearly opaque (thick skins).

From Vine to Glass

How Wine Is Made

1. Harvest

Grapes are picked at peak ripeness—timing is everything. Too early: tart and thin. Too late: flabby and over-sweet. In cooler regions (like NZ), harvest is March–April. In the Northern Hemisphere, it's August–October.

2. Crushing & Pressing

Grapes are gently crushed. For whites, juice separates from skins immediately. For reds, skins stay in for color and tannin extraction.

3. Fermentation

Yeast converts grape sugar into alcohol and CO₂. Takes 1–4 weeks. Cool fermentation (whites) preserves freshness; warmer fermentation (reds) extracts more color and flavor.

4. Aging

Stainless steel: Preserves bright fruit and acidity. Typical for Sauvignon Blanc—clean and crisp. Oak barrels: Add vanilla, spice, toast, butter (via malolactic (mal-oh-LAK-tik) — secondary fermentation). Classic for oaked Chardonnay. Time: A few months to several years depending on style.

5. Bottling

Wine may be filtered and fined (clarified), then bottled. Some wines improve further in the bottle—reds more so than whites.

Malolactic Fermentation: A secondary fermentation that converts sharp malic acid (green apple) into softer lactic acid (cream). This is what gives buttery Chardonnays their richness. Our Italian Chardonnay may or may not use this.

The Art of Wine Tasting

1. See

Tilt the glass against a white background. Color tells the story before you smell it—pale gold (young Chardonnay), translucent ruby (Pinot Noir), deep garnet (Primitivo).

2. Swirl & Smell

Swirling releases volatile aromatics. Dip your nose into the glass. First impressions: fruit? Flowers? Earth? Spice? The more you swirl, the more layers appear.

3. Sip & Taste

Take a small sip and draw air across the wine (gentle slurp). Notice sweetness (tip of tongue), acidity (sides), bitterness (back), tannin (gums and cheeks). The "mid-palate" is where complexity lives.

4. Savor the Finish

After swallowing, notice how long flavors linger. A 10-second finish is average; a 30+ second finish is exceptional. Does it evolve? Change? Leave you wanting more?

Pro Tip: There are no wrong answers. If you smell "grandma's kitchen" or "rainy day" instead of "blackcurrant" or "toast"—that's valid. Wine aromas trigger personal memories. Professional descriptors are just a shared language.

Sauvignon Blanc White Wine

New Zealand

Electric, vibrant, and unmistakable—New Zealand Sauvignon Blanc changed the wine world and put an entire country on the map.

Quick Facts

  • Grape: Sauvignon Blanc
  • Origin of Grape: Loire Valley & Bordeaux, France
  • This Wine: New Zealand
  • Style: Crisp, aromatic, high acidity
  • Fermentation: Stainless steel (unoaked)

What Makes It Special

New Zealand's cool climate and intense UV light (thinner ozone layer in the Southern Hemisphere) produce Sauvignon Blancs with explosive aromatics unlike anywhere else on earth.

The "Wild White" Grape

The name Sauvignon comes from the French word sauvage—"wild." It grew wild in the hedgerows of southwestern France for centuries before being cultivated.

1500s–1600s First documented in the Loire Valley. It thrives in Sancerre (sahn-SAIR) — Loire region and Pouilly-Fumé (pwee-YEE foo-MAY) — Loire region, becoming France's signature aromatic white grape.
1800s In Bordeaux, Sauvignon Blanc crosses naturally with Cabernet Franc to create Cabernet Sauvignon—the world's most planted red grape. Sauvignon Blanc is literally the parent of Cabernet.
1973 The first Sauvignon Blanc vines are planted in Marlborough, New Zealand. The wine world doesn't notice—yet.
1985 Cloudy Bay releases their first Marlborough Sauvignon Blanc. It becomes a sensation overnight—a style the world had never tasted. Explosive tropical fruit, lime, and herbaceous notes.

The Flavor Science: The "grassy" and "tropical" aromas come from volatile thiol (THY-ol) — aroma molecule compounds (3MH and 3MHA). NZ's UV light and cool nights concentrate these compounds to extraordinary levels. French versions are more mineral and restrained; NZ versions are a flavor explosion.

New Zealand's Wine Revolution

Why Marlborough?

Geography: At the northern tip of the South Island, Marlborough is a narrow valley between mountain ranges. Cool maritime winds funnel through, keeping temperatures low.

Sunlight: Long growing season with intense UV (up to 40% more than France). Grapes develop thick skins rich in aromatic compounds as a natural defense.

The Result: Wines with piercing acidity, explosive fruit, and incredible aromatic intensity that cannot be replicated anywhere else.

The Numbers

  • 1973: First vines planted in Marlborough
  • Today: 86% of NZ wine is Sauvignon Blanc
  • NZ is 6th largest wine exporter in the world by value
  • NZ Sauvignon Blanc outsells French Sancerre globally by 10 to 1

Record Speed: NZ went from zero wine reputation to global powerhouse in under 40 years—faster than any wine region in history.

This is a great moment to pour the Sauvignon Blanc. Ask everyone to look at the color first (very pale, green-tinged), then smell before sipping. The aromatics should be immediately explosive.

Sauvignon Blanc: Tasting Notes

Appearance

Pale straw to light gold with green-tinged highlights—brilliant clarity

Nose

Explosive aromas of passion fruit, grapefruit, lime zest, fresh-cut grass, gooseberry, and a hint of jalapeño

Palate

Racy acidity, zesty citrus, tropical fruit, green herbs, mineral finish. Refreshing and electric.

Finish

Crisp, clean, and refreshing with a lingering citrus zing

Pairing Ideas: Goat cheese, sushi, ceviche, grilled shrimp, salads with citrus vinaigrette, Thai cuisine, oysters

Ask the group what they smell—people often pick up on the grass, the tropical fruit, or sometimes the herbaceous/green pepper note. All valid! The jalapeño note (methoxypyrazines (meth-OX-ee-PEER-ah-zeens) — aroma compounds) is a signature of Sauvignon Blanc.
Chardonnay White Wine

Italy

The world's most popular white grape—a chameleon that reflects its winemaker and its home more than any other variety.

Quick Facts

  • Grape: Chardonnay
  • Origin: Burgundy, France
  • This Wine: Italy
  • Style: Elegant, medium-bodied
  • Rank: Most planted white grape on earth

The Chameleon Grape

Chardonnay has a relatively neutral flavor profile, making it a blank canvas. Oak gives it richness and butter. Steel keeps it crisp and bright. The winemaker's decisions matter more than almost any other grape.

The Queen of White Grapes

Chardonnay's homeland is Burgundy, France, where it produces some of the most expensive white wines on earth—think Montrachet (mohn-rah-SHAY) — famous vineyard ($300+/bottle) and Chablis (shah-BLEE) — Burgundy region.

Origins DNA analysis revealed Chardonnay is a natural cross between Pinot Noir and Gouais Blanc (GOO-ay BLAHNK)—a humble, nearly extinct peasant grape. Royalty meets common folk in one vine.
Medieval Era Cistercian (sis-TER-shun) monks in Burgundy discover that Chardonnay expresses terroir (tehr-WAHR) like no other grape—the same variety tastes completely different from one vineyard to the next, even a few rows apart.
1980s–1990s Heavily oaked, buttery California Chardonnay dominates. It becomes so popular that a backlash emerges: the "ABC" movement—Anything But Chardonnay.
Today The pendulum has swung back. Modern Chardonnays emphasize balance, minerality, and restraint. Italian and Burgundian styles lead this elegant renaissance.
The ABC movement is a fun anecdote—people got so sick of over-oaked, buttery Chardonnay in the '90s that they swore off the grape entirely. But the grape wasn't the problem; the winemaking was. Italian Chardonnay tends to be exactly what changed people's minds: fresh, balanced, not a butter bomb.

Chardonnay: The Italian Way

Italian vs. Californian

When most Americans think "Chardonnay," they think of the rich, buttery California style—heavy oak, malolactic fermentation, golden color.

Italian Chardonnay is a different animal. Grown primarily in the cooler north (Trentino-Alto Adige (tren-TEE-no AHL-toe AH-dee-jay) — Alpine north, Friuli (free-OO-lee) — northeast Italy, Veneto (VEH-neh-toe) — Venice region), it leans toward freshness, lemon zest, white flowers, and minerality.

Less oak (or none). Less butter. More elegance. Think "silk scarf" instead of "fur coat."

Why Italy Grows Chardonnay

Chardonnay has been in northern Italy since the 1800s. The cool Alpine foothills mirror Burgundy's climate—cold nights preserve acidity while warm days develop flavor.

Italy's Chardonnays rarely get the spotlight because Italy has 500+ native grapes. But many sommeliers consider Italian Chardonnay some of the best-value whites in the world.

Champagne Connection: Chardonnay is one of the three permitted grapes in Champagne (alongside Pinot Noir and Pinot Meunier (mun-YAY) — Champagne grape). A Champagne labeled Blanc de Blancs (blahnk duh BLAHNK) is 100% Chardonnay.

Great transition: "Now we're going to taste a Chardonnay that might change your mind about Chardonnay." Pour and ask people to compare the color to the Sauvignon Blanc—it should be slightly deeper gold.

Chardonnay: Tasting Notes

Appearance

Pale to medium gold—noticeably richer than the Sauvignon Blanc, reflecting more body and weight

Nose

Lemon, green apple, white peach, delicate white flowers, hints of almond, subtle mineral and stone

Palate

Medium-bodied with balanced acidity, citrus and stone fruit, subtle creaminess without heaviness, clean mineral finish

Finish

Elegant and lingering with notes of lemon peel and a whisper of toasted almond

Pairing Ideas: Roasted chicken, risotto, creamy pasta, mild fish, soft cheeses like brie, mushroom dishes, white pizza

Compare directly to the Sauvignon Blanc: "Notice how this has more weight, more roundness? That's the Chardonnay's fuller body. But it's still fresh—not heavy or buttery like a California Chard. That's the Italian touch."
Pinot Noir Red Wine

Monterey, California

Called "the heartbreak grape" for its difficulty to grow, Pinot Noir rewards patience with wines of extraordinary elegance and complexity.

Quick Facts

  • Grape: Pinot Noir
  • Origin of Grape: Burgundy, France
  • This Wine: Monterey County, California
  • Style: Light-to-medium body, silky tannins
  • Skin: Thin-skinned—lighter color than most reds

Why Monterey?

Monterey's long, cool growing season—Pacific Ocean fog funnels through the Salinas (sah-LEE-nahs) Valley—creates ideal conditions. Grapes ripen slowly, developing intense flavor while retaining bright acidity. It's one of the longest growing seasons in California.

The Heartbreak Grape

Pinot Noir is one of the oldest cultivated grape varieties—and the most demanding. Winemakers call it "the heartbreak grape" because it is notoriously difficult to grow well.

~100 AD Romans first document Pinot Noir in Burgundy. The name comes from French pin (pine)—its tight clusters resemble pinecones. One of the first grapes ever intentionally cultivated.
Medieval Era Cistercian and Benedictine monks elevate Pinot Noir to an art. They discover small soil differences produce dramatically different wines—the birth of Burgundy's cru (KROO) — vineyard ranking system. Some of these vineyard boundaries are still used 900 years later.
The Challenge Thin-skinned and genetically unstable, Pinot Noir is susceptible to frost, rot, mildew, and mutation. It demands cool climates, perfect drainage, and obsessive viticultural care. It punishes neglect.
DNA Legacy Pinot Noir is a parent grape of Chardonnay, Gamay (Beaujolais), and many others. It has over 1,000 known clonal variations—more than any other grape on earth.
Connect to the Chardonnay they just tasted: "Remember I said Chardonnay is a cross of Pinot Noir and Gouais Blanc (GOO-ay BLAHNK)? That means the white wine you just drank and the red you're about to taste are actually related. They're parent and child."

California Pinot Noir

The Sideways Effect

The 2004 film Sideways sparked a Pinot Noir boom—US sales surged 16% after its release. The character Miles delivered the famous line: "I am NOT drinking any Merlot!"

Merlot sales actually dropped 2% that year. Hollywood changed the American wine market overnight.

California Pinot Noir is typically fruit-forward and approachable compared to Burgundy's earthier, more restrained style. Monterey sits between the two—fruit with elegance.

The Monterey Terroir

The Salinas Valley: This 100-mile-long valley acts as a natural wind tunnel, channeling cold Pacific air inland every afternoon. Morning sun + afternoon fog = perfect Pinot conditions.

Cool Climate Advantage: The lengthy growing season (one of the longest in California) means grapes hang on the vine longer, developing complex flavors while maintaining crisp acidity.

  • Soil: Limestone and sandy loam—excellent drainage
  • Avg. Temperature: Highs of 70–75°F in growing season
  • Region II on the Winkler scale (same as Burgundy)
Pour the Pinot Noir. Ask everyone to hold it up to the light: "Notice how you can almost see through it? That transparency is because Pinot Noir has very thin skins—less pigment. Compare that to the Primitivo coming next, which will be nearly black."

Pinot Noir: Tasting Notes

Appearance

Translucent ruby—you can almost see through it. Lighter than most reds due to Pinot Noir's thin skins and low anthocyanin (an-tho-SY-uh-nin) — skin pigment content.

Nose

Red cherry, raspberry, strawberry, subtle earthy notes, hints of mushroom, dried herbs, and a touch of baking spice

Palate

Silky texture, bright red fruit, balanced acidity, soft tannins, cola and spice undertones, elegant weight

Finish

Elegant and lingering with a delicate interplay of fruit, earth, and gentle spice

Pairing Ideas: Salmon, duck, roasted turkey, mushroom dishes, gruyère (groo-YAIR) — Swiss cheese, charcuterie (shar-KOO-tuh-ree) — cured meats, lamb chops

Ask: "What's the biggest difference you notice between this red and the whites?" Most people will say texture—the tannins create a drying sensation on the gums. That's the skin contact we talked about.
Primitivo Red Wine

Puglia (POOL-yah), Italy

Italy's sun-drenched answer to bold, fruit-forward reds—and the genetic twin of California's Zinfandel.

Quick Facts

  • Grape: Primitivo
  • Region: Puglia (the heel of Italy's boot)
  • Style: Full-bodied, rich, high alcohol
  • Alias: Genetically identical to Zinfandel
  • Character: Jammy, bold, warming

The Zinfandel Connection

DNA testing in the 1990s proved Primitivo and Zinfandel are the same grape. Both trace to Crljenak Kaštelanski, a Croatian variety. Same DNA, different continents, completely different wines.

The Grape with Three Names

Primitivo's history is a detective story spanning three continents and centuries of mystery.

Ancient Roots The grape originates in Croatia as Crljenak Kaštelanski (pronounced "tsurl-YEH-nak kash-teh-LAHN-skee"). It crosses the Adriatic Sea to Italy's Puglia, likely brought by monks or maritime traders.
1700s Italian priest Don Francesco Filippo Indellicati (in-del-ee-KAH-tee) notices this grape ripens earlier than others. He names it Primitivo (pree-mee-TEE-vo)—from Latin primativus (pree-mah-TEE-voos), "first to ripen." Not "primitive" in the English sense.
1820s The grape arrives in America (likely via Austrian botanical collections) and is planted in California. Americans call it Zinfandel—origin of that name remains debated.
1994–2001 UC Davis professor Carole Meredith uses DNA fingerprinting to prove Zinfandel = Primitivo = Crljenak Kaštelanski. One of the great wine mysteries, solved by genetics.
This is a crowd-pleaser story. "How many of you like Zinfandel? You're about to taste the same grape—but grown in Italy, in a completely different climate, by Italian winemakers. Same DNA, different expression. Like identical twins raised in different countries."

Puglia: Italy's Sun-Drenched South

The Region

Puglia (POOL-yah) (also called "Apulia" (ah-POOL-yah)) is the heel of Italy's boot, stretching into the Mediterranean. It's one of Italy's sunniest, warmest regions—flat, arid, and sun-baked.

For decades, Puglia was Italy's bulk wine factory—quantity over quality. Tanker trucks of anonymous red were shipped north to "improve" thinner wines from cooler regions.

Since the 1990s, a quality revolution has transformed Puglia. Winemakers discovered that old-vine Primitivo—some vines 80+ years old—produces wines of incredible depth and concentration.

Primitivo vs. Zinfandel

  • Italian Primitivo: Tends toward dried fruit, fig, chocolate, earthiness. More rustic and Mediterranean.
  • California Zinfandel: Tends toward jammy fresh fruit, bold spice, higher alcohol. More exuberant and "sunny."

The difference is terroir and winemaking philosophy. Italian Primitivo is restrained by tradition; California Zinfandel is amplified by ambition.

High Alcohol Warning: Primitivo naturally produces high sugar levels, which means high alcohol—often 14–15%+. This creates the "warming" sensation in the finish. That's the alcohol, not spice (though spice is there too).

Pour the Primitivo last. Ask everyone to compare the color side-by-side with the Pinot Noir. The Primitivo should be dramatically darker and more opaque—thick skins = deep color. This is the skin contact lesson in action.

Primitivo: Tasting Notes

Appearance

Deep garnet to inky purple—opaque and intense; you cannot see through it. Dramatic contrast to the translucent Pinot Noir.

Nose

Ripe blackberry, plum jam, dried fig, black pepper, vanilla, cocoa, and a hint of leather

Palate

Full-bodied and velvety, rich dark fruit, baking spices (cinnamon, clove), smooth tannins, warming alcohol

Finish

Long and warming with lingering dark fruit, chocolate, gentle spice, and a touch of dried herb

Pairing Ideas: BBQ ribs, lamb shank, aged pecorino (peh-koh-REE-no) — sheep's milk cheese, wood-fired pizza with sausage, dark chocolate, hearty ragout (ra-GOO) — thick stew

Ask: "What's the biggest difference you feel between this and the Pinot Noir?" Most will say weight/body. The Primitivo should feel like a warm hug while the Pinot felt like silk. Both are red wine—but completely different experiences.

Key Takeaways

  • Wine is ancient: Over 8,000 years of history in every glass—older than the wheel
  • Color comes from skin contact: All grape juice is essentially clear; time on skins determines color
  • Grapes have families: Pinot Noir is Chardonnay's parent; Sauvignon Blanc gave birth to Cabernet Sauvignon
  • Same grape, different wine: Primitivo and Zinfandel share identical DNA but express completely differently
  • Old World vs. New World: Tradition and terroir versus innovation and fruit—tonight you tasted both
  • The winemaker matters: Oak vs. steel, skin contact time, aging—every choice shapes the final wine
  • Trust your palate: There are no wrong answers—if you enjoy it, it's a good wine
